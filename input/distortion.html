The Distortion Lesson

(Jonathan is onstage with his electric guitar, ready to introduce the concept. Becky looks on with concern.)

Jonathan: (enthusiastically) Alright, everyone! Today, we’re going to teach the children about something really cool in music: distortion!

Becky: (worried) Distortion? But isn’t that a bit… rebellious?

Jonathan: (reassuringly) Well, personally, I think it’s better for kids to learn about distortion in a safe, controlled environment while they’re parents are here if they need them. Because, like it or not, they are going to encounter distortion out there in the real world. We can’t protect them forever.

(Becky nods, still a bit apprehensive. Jonathan starts with the first riff.)

Jonathan: Let’s start with something mild. Here’s a classic riff from the late ’50s called “Rumble.” It’s considered one of the first ever songs to feature distorted guitar.

(He plays the riff from “Rumble” by Link Wray. Becky listens and then nods in approval.)

Becky: (getting into it) Okay, that wasn’t so bad. What’s next?

Jonathan: (smiling) Time to kick it up a notch with “Born to Be Wild” from the late ’60s. Remember, kids, if you’re confused or overwhelmed, you can always turn to a parent. Everyone ready?

(He plays the opening riff from “Born to Be Wild” by Steppenwolf. Becky starts to move to the music, clearly enjoying it.)

Becky: (excitedly) Oh, I like that! More, more, more!

Jonathan: (Uncertain) Remember, this is for the kids, right?

Becky: (excitedly) Yeah, yeah, the kids, whatever. I mean… the kids want more distortion! Don’t you, kids!? (gestures to get the to cheer) See?

Jonathan: (Uncertain) Um, ok, sure. Here’s some Led Zeppelin you might like.

(He plays the riff from “Whole Lotta Love.” Becky is now fully rocking out, hair all over the place? Horns gesture, etc. Jonathan stops and looks at Becky uncertainty)

Becky: What?

Jonathan: I think that might be enough distortion for now.

Becky: But we still have to play [Green Goo]!

Jonathan: Oh, right!
